# Identity-Aware OT Architecture

The preceding section established that authorization in operational technology environments has structural limitations that cannot be resolved by improving individual components in isolation. Network segmentation can be made more granular; controller-local credentials can be better managed; jump host logging can be made more thorough. None of these improvements address the underlying issue: when authorization logic is distributed across independent systems with no shared model, shared identity, or shared audit infrastructure, the properties that distributed authorization makes difficult — consistent policy, end-to-end attribution, centralized observability — remain difficult regardless of how well each individual component is maintained.

This section describes what authorization looks like when it is treated as a system-level concern. The architecture is conceptual and generalized: it describes a model, not a product. Different deployments will implement subsets of this model, at different levels of maturity, with different technologies, under different operational constraints. The model is intended to be directional — a coherent target that can be approached incrementally — not a specification that requires full implementation before delivering value.

---

## Architectural Overview

The central organizing principle of identity-aware authorization in OT environments is the separation of three functions that are conflated in protocol-centric models: identity verification, policy evaluation, and policy enforcement.

In a protocol-centric model, these functions are collapsed into a single mechanism — network membership — that performs all three simultaneously and coarsely. A device on the trusted segment is implicitly verified, implicitly authorized, and implicitly permitted to enforce its own communication. There is no separate identity layer, no separate policy evaluation step, and no independent enforcement point.

Separating these functions creates a more articulated architecture with distinct components and distinct responsibilities:

**The identity layer** is responsible for issuing and verifying credentials for all principals — operators, devices, services, and automated processes — that interact with the system. It is the authoritative source of verified identity. Other components rely on the identity layer but do not perform identity verification themselves.

**The policy evaluation layer** is responsible for receiving authorization requests — structured descriptions of who is asking to do what to which resource — and applying policy to determine whether the request is permitted. The policy evaluation layer does not verify identity (it relies on the identity layer for that) and does not directly enforce decisions (it produces decisions that enforcement points apply). It is the authoritative source of policy outcomes.

**The enforcement layer** is distributed: enforcement points sit at trust boundaries and at the edges of operational zones, applying authorization decisions to traffic attempting to cross those boundaries. Enforcement points may consult the policy evaluation layer in real time or operate against a locally cached copy of policy. They do not independently determine policy; they apply decisions produced by the policy evaluation layer.

**The audit layer** receives structured records of authorization decisions from enforcement points and from the policy evaluation layer and persists them in a form that supports later review. It is independent of the operational layers — it does not affect authorization outcomes but provides the observability necessary to understand and verify them.

This separation does not require that each layer be implemented as a distinct service, or that all layers be centrally hosted. It requires that the responsibilities be clearly allocated — that policy evaluation is not duplicated inconsistently across enforcement points, that enforcement points do not make their own identity determinations, and that audit records are generated by a shared mechanism rather than separately by each component.

## Subjects, Resources, and Actions

The authorization model is built on three primitives that must be defined consistently across the deployment: the subject making a request, the resource being requested, and the action being performed.

**Subjects** are the principals — operators, devices, services, automated processes — whose requests are subject to authorization. In OT contexts, subjects include human operators interacting through workstations or HMI terminals, technician accounts used for configuration and maintenance, vendor service accounts accessing equipment for support purposes, controller-to-controller communication where one device acts on behalf of another, and automated systems such as energy management platforms or analytics pipelines that consume OT data. Each subject must have a verifiable identity: a credential that the identity layer can authenticate and that carries enough information for the policy engine to evaluate applicable policy.

**Resources** are the system objects to which access is requested: individual BAS controller points, device configuration interfaces, telemetry data streams, system configuration parameters, audit records, or other operational assets. For policy evaluation to be meaningful, resources must be described with sufficient specificity and consistent naming across systems. A policy that grants a specific operator access to "HVAC setpoints in Building A, Floor 3" requires that the resource naming system can represent that scope. Resource descriptors must be stable — a resource that changes its identifier under operational conditions creates ambiguity in both policy application and audit records.

**Actions** are the discrete operations requested on a resource: reads, writes, commands, subscriptions, configuration changes, acknowledgments. The granularity of action definition has a direct effect on the expressiveness of the policy model. A system that recognizes only "read" and "write" can express that an operator can read sensor data but not write setpoints. A system that recognizes "read," "write-setpoint," "write-configuration," and "issue-override-command" can express distinctions that are operationally meaningful in a BAS context — a maintenance technician may be permitted to write configuration but not issue override commands; an operator may be permitted to write setpoints within a defined range but not issue configuration changes.

The triplet of subject, resource, and action forms the basis of every authorization request. The policy engine receives this triplet along with relevant contextual attributes and returns a decision. Everything else in the authorization architecture exists to produce this triplet reliably and to enforce the decisions that result from it.

## Policy Evaluation Model

The policy evaluation model defines how authorization decisions are made. At a minimum, the policy engine receives a structured request containing the authenticated identity of the subject, the resource descriptor, and the requested action. It applies the set of policies that are applicable to that request and returns a decision — typically `allow`, `deny`, or `not applicable` when no policy covers the request.

The simplest policy models are role-based: policies grant specific actions on specific resources to subjects that hold specific roles. A role-based policy might express "operators may read any sensor point in their assigned zone" or "maintenance technicians may write configuration parameters to any controller of type X." Role-based models are tractable to administer and reason about, and they cover the majority of access patterns in a well-structured BAS deployment.

More expressive models — attribute-based access control — allow policies to incorporate arbitrary attributes of the subject, resource, action, or environment. An attribute-based policy might express "operators may issue setpoint override commands only during a declared maintenance window and only for controllers in their assigned zone." The expressiveness comes at the cost of policy complexity: attribute-based policies are harder to audit for correctness and harder to reason about when they interact.

In practice, the appropriate policy model for a given deployment depends on the granularity of access distinctions the deployment requires. Starting with role-based policies and introducing attribute-based conditions where operational requirements demand them is a more maintainable approach than attempting to build a comprehensive attribute-based model from the outset.

A critical property of the policy evaluation model is determinism. Given the same input — subject identity, resource, action, context — the policy engine must always produce the same output. Non-deterministic policy evaluation is operationally hazardous: operators who cannot predict the outcome of an authorization request cannot reliably perform their work, and incident investigators who cannot reproduce the authorization state that existed at the time of an event cannot interpret the audit record. Determinism requires that policy changes be versioned and that the policy version in effect at any point in time be auditable.

## Enforcement Points

Enforcement points are the components that apply authorization decisions to operational traffic. They are distinct from the policy evaluation layer — they do not determine policy, they apply decisions that have already been made. This separation is architecturally significant: it means that the policy evaluation layer can be updated, tested, and verified independently of the enforcement infrastructure, and that enforcement points can be deployed at the edges of the operational environment without embedding complex policy logic into each one.

In an OT context, enforcement points sit at trust boundary crossings: at the gateway between the field device zone and the supervisory zone; at the ingress point for remote operator sessions; at the interface between the supervisory layer and enterprise-connected systems; and, where feasible, at the protocol adapter layer where field-device communications are normalized.

Each enforcement point intercepts a request, constructs an authorization request from the available context, submits it to the policy evaluation layer (or evaluates it against a local policy cache), receives a decision, and applies the decision — either permitting the request to proceed, rejecting it, or logging it for later review where audit-only enforcement is appropriate during a transition period.

The granularity of enforcement is bounded by what the enforcement point can observe. A gateway that receives a BACnet command and forwards or rejects it can enforce authorization at the command level only if it can decode the command to extract the resource and action. A gateway that treats BACnet as an opaque byte stream can enforce only at the connection or session level. This is one of the operational reasons that protocol adapters — which perform normalization — are positioned adjacent to enforcement points in the architecture.

Not every enforcement point needs to operate in real time against the policy engine. In environments where the policy engine is not always reachable, enforcement points operate against locally cached policy. The architecture must define what cached policy means, how it is populated and maintained, and what happens when cached policy expires. These are operational parameters that vary by deployment.

## Identity Propagation Across Trust Boundaries

When a request passes through multiple components — an operator command that originates at an HMI, passes through a supervisory system, crosses a gateway, and reaches a field controller — the identity of the original requester must be preserved at each step. Without identity propagation, the enforcement point at each boundary can only observe the identity of the immediate upstream component, not the identity of the human principal who initiated the request. The result is that authorization decisions at inner boundaries are made on behalf of systems, not operators.

Identity propagation requires a mechanism for carrying authenticated identity context through the request chain in a form that each component can verify. The implementation approach depends on the protocols and components involved. Where modern, authenticated protocols are in use between components, identity context can be carried in signed tokens attached to the request. Where legacy protocols are in use, the protocol adapter that normalizes the request is responsible for attaching verified identity context derived from the authenticated session in which the request arrived.

The identity context carried in a propagated request must be signed or otherwise integrity-protected so that intermediate components cannot substitute or modify it. An intermediary that strips identity context or replaces it with its own identity breaks the propagation chain in a way that is difficult to detect from the audit record.

Propagation does not require that every component in the chain be fully identity-aware. It requires that the enforcement points at each trust boundary crossing receive identity context that is valid and attributable to the original requester, even if the components between those enforcement points do not participate in the identity model. This is a more achievable requirement in environments with legacy intermediate components than requiring every component to natively support identity protocols.

## Protocol Abstraction and Adapters

Field-level OT protocols — BACnet, Modbus, LonWorks, and others — were not designed with the authorization model described in this section in mind. They carry no native concept of authenticated subject identity, no standard mechanism for attaching identity context to messages, and no consistent representation of resources or actions that maps cleanly to a policy model.

Protocol adapters address this gap by normalizing field-protocol messages into the shared representation used by the authorization and audit infrastructure. Each adapter receives field-protocol messages, parses them according to the protocol's semantics, extracts the relevant resource and action information, attaches the authenticated identity of the session in which the message arrived, and submits the resulting normalized representation to the enforcement point for authorization evaluation.

This normalization serves several purposes. It isolates protocol-specific logic in the adapter layer, keeping it out of the policy engine and audit pipeline. It allows the policy model to express policies in terms of BAS concepts — device points, control actions, data subscriptions — rather than protocol-specific message formats. It allows the audit pipeline to receive consistent event schemas regardless of the originating protocol. And it allows the authorization infrastructure to remain stable as protocols are updated or new protocols are added, because new protocol support requires only a new adapter, not changes to the shared components.

The adapter layer does not remove all protocol-specific complexity. Adapters must be implemented and maintained for each protocol in use, and they must handle the operational semantics of each protocol correctly to produce accurate resource and action representations. A BACnet adapter that misinterprets a priority array write as a standard property write would produce an inaccurate authorization request that could result in incorrect policy application. Protocol adapter correctness is a precondition for authorization model correctness.

Not all field-device communication is amenable to adapter-based normalization. Communication that occurs below the adapter layer — direct serial connections, maintenance interfaces accessed outside the normal communication path, vendor diagnostic tools — bypasses the authorization infrastructure entirely. These are documented gaps that require compensating controls rather than architectural workarounds.

## Centralized Auditability

An audit pipeline that is independent of the systems being audited is a prerequisite for meaningful accountability. When each system generates its own logs, in its own schema, under its own administrative control, the resulting set of records is inconsistent, incomplete, and difficult to correlate. More significantly, a log generated by the system that is itself the subject of an investigation can be influenced by the same access that produced the events being investigated.

The authorization architecture generates audit records at a specific and well-defined point: when an enforcement point applies an authorization decision. The decision record includes the authenticated identity of the subject, the resource descriptor, the requested action, the policy version in effect, the decision outcome, and a timestamp. This record is emitted from the enforcement point to the audit pipeline, independent of whether the underlying operation succeeds or fails.

Generating audit records at the authorization decision point rather than at the operational event point provides a more complete record in two ways. First, it captures denied requests — events that did not result in operational activity but that are security-relevant. Second, it captures the authorization reasoning — the policy that applied, the identity that was presented — not just the outcome. This makes the audit record interpretable without requiring reconstruction of the authorization state that existed at the time.

For the audit record to be trustworthy, it must be written to a store that is separate from the operational components, that does not permit modification of existing records, and that is under administrative control distinct from the control of the OT operational systems. These properties — independence, immutability, and administrative separation — are the conditions under which an audit record can be relied upon for incident investigation and compliance review.

The audit pipeline must also handle the operational reality that enforcement points are distributed and may not always have reliable connectivity to central audit infrastructure. Enforcement points must buffer audit events locally and deliver them durably when connectivity is restored, rather than dropping events when the audit pipeline is temporarily unreachable. An audit record that is missing events due to transient connectivity issues is an incomplete record, and its incompleteness may not be detectable from the record itself.

## Control Plane and Data Plane Separation

The authorization infrastructure — policy evaluation, identity management, policy distribution, audit collection — constitutes a control plane that is distinct from the data plane carrying operational workloads. This separation is both an organizational principle and a practical engineering requirement.

As an organizational principle, the separation ensures that changes to authorization policy, identity configuration, or audit infrastructure are treated as distinct operational activities from changes to the OT systems they govern. A policy change is a control-plane event; a setpoint adjustment is a data-plane event. They require different review processes, different administrative access, and different audit treatment.

As an engineering requirement, the separation ensures that control-plane communications — policy distribution, authorization request and response, audit event collection — are not routed through the same paths as operational workloads. Control-plane traffic has different latency tolerance, different volume characteristics, and different failure mode requirements than data-plane traffic. Commingling them creates coupling that makes each harder to reason about independently.

Control-plane separation also limits the blast radius of certain failure modes. If the policy evaluation service becomes temporarily unavailable, the data plane continues to operate against cached policy, and the failure is isolated to the control plane. If a data-plane component is compromised, its ability to influence the control plane is limited by the architectural separation between them.

In OT environments, strict physical separation of control and data plane networks may not always be feasible. Logical separation — distinct communication paths, distinct access controls, distinct audit treatment — is the minimum. Physical separation should be pursued where the deployment environment supports it.

## Offline and Resilient Authorization

One of the most operationally significant requirements of the authorization architecture is that it must support continued safe operation when control-plane services are unavailable. A policy engine that is unreachable, an identity provider that is experiencing downtime, or a network partition that isolates an edge site from central infrastructure — any of these conditions must produce behavior that is defined, predictable, and operationally acceptable.

The primary mechanism for offline authorization is local policy caching. Enforcement points maintain a local copy of the policies applicable to the traffic they govern, populated by periodic synchronization with the policy evaluation layer. When the policy layer is unreachable, the enforcement point evaluates requests against the local cache. This provides continued authorization capability without requiring real-time connectivity to the policy infrastructure.

Local caching introduces several operational parameters that must be explicitly defined per deployment: the maximum age of cached policy before it is considered stale, the behavior when cached policy expires and the policy engine is still unreachable, and the mechanism for invalidating cached policy when urgent policy changes are required (such as revocation of a compromised credential). These parameters involve tradeoffs between security properties and operational continuity that are specific to each deployment context and must be determined by the operators of that deployment, not by the architecture.

Identity validation presents a different challenge. Credentials presented by operators or devices can be validated against cached credential material — certificate chains, token signatures, revocation lists — for a bounded period. If connectivity is not restored before cached credential material expires, the enforcement point must decide whether to continue accepting credentials it cannot fully validate or to require re-authentication when connectivity is restored. This decision is another deployment-specific operational parameter.

Beyond caching, the architecture must define behavior for enforcement points that experience internal failure — crashes, resource exhaustion, configuration errors. The choice between failing closed (denying all requests) and failing open (permitting all requests) is a fundamental safety and security tradeoff. In most contexts, failing closed is the correct default; in some OT contexts, failing closed under certain conditions could prevent responses to physical emergencies. Acceptable failure behavior must be specified per enforcement point, per deployment, with explicit rationale, and must be tested rather than assumed.

## Context-Aware Authorization Decisions

Static role assignments capture a baseline of access entitlements — what a given principal is permitted under normal operating conditions. Many operationally meaningful distinctions require conditions that static roles cannot express.

Context-aware authorization extends the policy model to incorporate attributes that vary over time and across conditions: the current time, the current operational mode of the building or site, the physical location of the requesting device, the current state of a supervised asset, whether a maintenance window has been declared, or whether an emergency condition has been activated. These contextual attributes allow policies to express distinctions like "maintenance technicians may write controller configuration only during a declared maintenance window" or "override commands may be issued only by operators with site-lead role during an active alarm condition."

For context-aware policies to be reliable, the contextual attributes they reference must themselves be trustworthy. An attribute that says "maintenance window is active" must be set by a process that is itself access-controlled and audited; otherwise it is a mechanism for circumventing the access controls it is supposed to condition. The trustworthiness of contextual inputs is as important as the correctness of the policies that reference them.

Context-aware authorization adds expressiveness to the policy model but also adds complexity. Policies that combine role conditions with multiple contextual conditions are harder to reason about and harder to test than pure role-based policies. The risk of unintended interactions between contextual conditions — where two conditions that are each individually reasonable produce an unexpected combined result — increases with the number of conditions in play. Context should be introduced into the policy model where it materially improves the precision of access decisions, not as a default feature of every policy.

## Architectural Tradeoffs and Constraints

The architecture described in this section provides capabilities that protocol-centric models cannot: consistent policy across heterogeneous systems, end-to-end identity attribution, centralized observability, and an audit record that supports meaningful review. These capabilities come at a cost.

**Latency.** Submitting an authorization request to a policy evaluation layer adds latency to every operation that crosses an enforcement point. In most BAS contexts, the latency introduced by a policy evaluation round-trip — typically measured in milliseconds for a well-provisioned evaluation service — is within acceptable bounds for supervisory-layer operations. For time-critical control loops operating at the field device level, even small latency additions may be unacceptable. The architecture accommodates this by positioning enforcement at points where latency can be tolerated and allowing field-level communications to operate within a segment that has already been authorized at the boundary crossing — rather than evaluating every individual field-protocol message.

**Operational complexity.** An identity-aware authorization architecture introduces infrastructure that must be operated, maintained, and monitored: the identity provider, the policy evaluation service, the enforcement components, and the audit pipeline. Each of these is a component that can fail, that requires updates, and that requires operational expertise to administer correctly. The operational burden of this infrastructure is real and should be evaluated against the operational burden of the distributed, fragmented authorization it replaces.

**Legacy device coverage.** Field devices that communicate over unauthenticated protocols and cannot participate in the identity model are not directly covered by the authorization architecture. They are covered at the boundary level — the gateway and enforcement point that control access to their segment — but not at the device level. This is a structural limitation for environments with large legacy device populations, and it should be acknowledged rather than minimized. The architecture improves the security posture of these environments without fully addressing the device-level gap.

**Partial deployment.** The authorization architecture does not require universal deployment to deliver value. An environment in which enforcement points are deployed at the primary trust boundaries — between the supervisory zone and enterprise networks, at remote access ingress points — is meaningfully more secure and more auditable than one with no enforcement points, even if field-level enforcement is not yet in place. Partial deployment is a realistic adoption path and should be treated as an achievable intermediate state, not as an incomplete implementation of the full architecture.

**New failure modes.** Every component added to the authorization path is a component that can fail. The policy evaluation service, the identity provider, and the audit pipeline each introduce availability requirements that do not exist in protocol-centric models. Designing for availability — through redundancy, caching, and defined failure behavior — is not optional. It is a first-order engineering requirement of the authorization infrastructure.

## Summary

The identity-aware authorization architecture described in this section separates what protocol-centric models collapse: identity verification, policy evaluation, and enforcement. By giving each function its own infrastructure — the identity layer, the policy evaluation layer, distributed enforcement points — and connecting them with well-defined interfaces, the architecture enables consistent policy across heterogeneous systems, end-to-end attribution of actions to authenticated principals, and a centralized audit record that supports meaningful operational review.

Protocol adapters absorb the protocol-specific complexity of field-level OT communication, normalizing requests into a shared representation that the authorization model can reason about without being coupled to any specific field protocol. Local policy caching makes the architecture operationally viable in environments where connectivity to central services is intermittent. Separation of control plane and data plane limits the scope of both failure modes and security events.

The architecture is not a universal solution. It requires infrastructure that must be operated and maintained. It does not cover device-level authorization for legacy field hardware. It does not eliminate the need for network segmentation. It introduces latency that must be accommodated in enforcement point placement. And it must be deployed against real OT operational workflows, with their specific maintenance windows, break-glass requirements, shift-change patterns, and availability constraints.

What the architecture provides is a direction: a coherent model for moving from a system where authorization is an implicit consequence of network topology toward one where it is an explicit, observable, and consistently enforced system property.
