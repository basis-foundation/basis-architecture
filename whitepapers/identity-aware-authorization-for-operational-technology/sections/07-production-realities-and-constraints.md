# Production Realities and Constraints

The architecture described in Sections 04 and 05 is designed around a set of structural properties: centralized policy evaluation, distributed enforcement, identity propagation across trust boundaries, and a local policy cache that supports offline operation. Section 06 documented where the BASIS PoC validated these properties at limited scope and where it intentionally deferred the engineering challenges that production deployment would introduce. This section takes up that deferred work directly.

The distance between a coherent architectural design and a deployable production system in OT environments is not primarily a gap in technical sophistication. It is a gap in operational discipline — the unglamorous but consequential work of defining failure modes, managing credential lifecycles, coordinating policy changes across distributed enforcement points, and providing operators with tools that do not interfere with the continuity requirements of the systems they govern. These concerns are not edge cases. In production OT deployments, they are the dominant engineering problems.

The following analysis does not argue that the architecture is wrong or that production deployment is infeasible. It argues that production deployment is substantially harder than the conceptual description implies, that many of the hardest problems are operational rather than purely technical, and that real deployments will require negotiated tradeoffs between security, continuity, latency, and operational workflow — tradeoffs that the architecture must accommodate rather than assume away.

## High Availability and Operational Continuity

The identity provider and the policy engine are the components that all enforcement points depend on for session establishment and policy synchronization. In the conceptual architecture, the local policy cache at each edge enforcement point provides resilience against temporary unavailability of the policy engine — enforcement continues against cached policy when the central engine is unreachable. But the cache only addresses one of several availability dependencies. A jump host that cannot reach the identity provider to validate operator tokens cannot admit new sessions, regardless of what the local policy cache holds. An enforcement point that cannot establish its own authenticated channel to the policy engine cannot receive policy updates or validate that its cached policy remains authoritative.

High availability design for these central components is more complex than it first appears. Replicating the policy engine across multiple instances requires that all instances share policy state consistently — a requirement that introduces the same distributed coordination problems the architecture was designed to avoid at the enforcement-point layer. An active-active policy engine cluster must resolve questions about how policy updates are applied consistently across all instances, how in-flight authorization requests are handled during a failover event, and how enforcement points determine which instance to contact when multiple are available. An active-passive configuration avoids some coordination complexity but requires that enforcement points and sessions handle the failover interval — the window during which the active instance has failed and the passive has not yet assumed its role — without losing or mishandling in-progress requests.

The identity provider carries similar requirements with different operational constraints. Identity providers used in production enterprise IAM deployments have well-established HA patterns — clustered deployments backed by replicated databases, with load balancers distributing authentication requests. Translating these patterns to an OT deployment context requires attention to the administrative and network isolation requirements of the OT environment. A Keycloak cluster deployed to support OT authorization in an air-gapped or limited-connectivity site operates under different assumptions than the same cluster deployed in a cloud-hosted enterprise context. The HA design must account for the specific connectivity topology of the deployment rather than adopting cloud-native patterns that may not apply.

The audit log store also carries availability requirements that are distinct from the operational OT systems it covers. An audit store that is temporarily unavailable does not interrupt operational continuity — enforcement can proceed against cached policy, and audit events can buffer locally — but it degrades the real-time visibility that the audit record is intended to provide. Audit events that buffer locally during a central audit store outage will eventually deliver, but the delivery order may not reflect the chronological order of the events. Any audit analysis or alerting that depends on real-time event delivery must account for this delivery model.

ISA/IEC 62443-3-3 identifies availability as a security property that OT systems must maintain alongside integrity and confidentiality [ISA/IEC 62443-3-3, 2013]. This framing is important: authorization infrastructure that is designed to improve OT security but that itself becomes a source of operational unavailability is counterproductive. High availability requirements for the authorization layer must be developed in coordination with the operational continuity requirements of the systems it governs, not derived independently from IT infrastructure norms.

## Offline Authorization and Policy Distribution

The local policy cache is the architecture's primary mechanism for maintaining authorization capability during connectivity gaps between edge enforcement points and the Central Services Zone. Its design implications are more operationally complex than the cache abstraction suggests.

Policy distribution requires a defined format, a defined delivery mechanism, and a defined consistency model. The policy engine must produce a representation of applicable policy that is compact enough to transmit over potentially constrained links, structured enough for enforcement points to evaluate efficiently, and versioned in a way that allows enforcement points to determine whether their local copy is current. None of these requirements is technically novel — policy distribution is a solved class of problem in enterprise IAM contexts — but the constraints of OT network topologies complicate implementation in ways that enterprise-oriented tooling does not always anticipate.

Edge sites in OT deployments may connect to central services over narrow, unreliable, or metered links. A policy distribution mechanism that assumes high-bandwidth, low-latency connectivity will behave unpredictably in environments with high-latency satellite uplinks, cellular backhaul with limited monthly data caps, or serial links that predate the Ethernet infrastructure the authorization system assumes. Policy format design must account for the worst-case connectivity that will exist in the deployment, not the average case.

Partial policy distribution introduces additional complexity. If an enforcement point can receive some policy updates but not others — because the delivery mechanism does not provide transactional delivery guarantees — the local cache may contain a mixture of current and stale policy from different parts of the policy tree. Evaluating authorization requests against such a mixture can produce outcomes that are consistent with neither the old policy nor the new policy as coherent wholes. Policy distribution must either guarantee atomic delivery of complete policy snapshots or define a principled approach to incremental updates that the enforcement point can apply without creating inconsistent intermediate states.

The staleness limit — the maximum age at which cached policy is considered current enough to enforce — is a deployment-specific parameter, but the operational implications of setting it incorrectly in either direction deserve explicit analysis. A staleness limit that is too short relative to the frequency of connectivity gaps will cause the enforcement point to reach its limit during normal operations, forcing it into a defined failure behavior before any connectivity problem has occurred. A staleness limit that is too long extends the window during which a revoked credential or changed policy may still be honored at edge enforcement points. NIST SP 800-82 Rev. 3 notes that OT environments must carefully balance security posture against the operational availability requirements of industrial processes [NIST SP 800-82, Rev. 3]. Staleness limit design is exactly this kind of balance — it cannot be resolved by the architecture alone, and it must be revisited as the operational characteristics of the deployment change over time.

## Policy Cache Invalidation and Synchronization

Cache invalidation is among the best-known hard problems in distributed systems, and the local policy cache introduces it into OT environments in forms that have direct operational consequences. The architecture described in Section 04 accepts this as a structural tradeoff: enforcement points operating against locally cached policy exist in a state of eventual consistency with the central policy engine, with the synchronization window defining the maximum observable divergence between the distributed state held at edge sites and the authoritative policy state maintained centrally.

The most operationally significant invalidation scenario is credential revocation. When an operator's credentials are revoked — because they have left the organization, because a vendor engagement has ended, or because a security investigation has begun — the revocation event must propagate to all enforcement points that might honor those credentials before the operator's next access attempt. In an enterprise IAM context, revocation propagation is typically fast relative to the human access cycle: a revocation event in a directory service propagates to authentication infrastructure within seconds or minutes. In an OT deployment where edge enforcement points synchronize policy on a schedule, or where connectivity to the central policy engine is intermittent, the propagation window may be measured in hours or longer.

The practical consequence is that a revoked operator credential may remain valid at a cached enforcement point until the next successful policy synchronization. Whether this is an acceptable operational risk depends on the circumstances that triggered the revocation and the operational sensitivity of the resources the enforcement point governs. For a credential revoked because an operator has changed roles, the residual window of validity may be acceptable. For a credential revoked as part of an active incident response, it may not be. Authorization architectures that are deployed into environments with high-sensitivity resources must make this tradeoff explicit and define procedures for forcing immediate cache invalidation when the staleness window is operationally unacceptable.

Forcing immediate cache invalidation in a partially disconnected environment is not straightforward. An enforcement point that is disconnected from the central policy engine cannot receive an invalidation signal. The approaches available — extending connectivity specifically for revocation events, operating with a separate out-of-band invalidation channel, or maintaining emergency revocation tokens that enforcement points check before honoring any cached credential — each introduce architectural complexity and operational dependencies that must be designed and tested before they are needed.

Cache divergence at scale introduces a different kind of consistency problem. When a large deployment includes dozens of enforcement points, each synchronizing on its own schedule and each experiencing different connectivity conditions, the policy state across the fleet at any given moment may be heterogeneous. Some enforcement points may be operating on policy that is minutes old; others may be operating on policy that is hours or days old. A policy change that adjusts the scope of a vendor's access will take effect at some enforcement points immediately and at others only on their next successful synchronization. The operational behavior of the system during the propagation window is neither fully governed by the old policy nor fully governed by the new one — it is governed by a mixture whose composition depends on which enforcement points have synchronized and which have not.

This heterogeneous policy state is not a failure condition. It is the normal operational state of a distributed authorization system with imperfect connectivity — an instance of bounded inconsistency that the cache design accepts by construction. Operators and policy administrators must understand it as such and design policy changes to be safe during the propagation window, not only after full propagation has completed.

Many production deployments will operate in this condition not temporarily but persistently. For sites with genuinely unreliable connectivity to central services, the synchronization window is not a brief transitional state but a steady-state operating condition. The architecture does not eliminate this inconsistency; it bounds it. Whether the bound is acceptable depends on the nature of the policies in question and the operational sensitivity of the resources they govern — a judgment that belongs to the deployment, not to the architecture. Accepting bounded policy inconsistency in exchange for operational continuity is a legitimate deployment choice, not an architectural failure.

## Latency and Deterministic Operations

OT environments operate across a spectrum of latency requirements. Supervisory-layer operations — operator commands submitted through the jump host and routed toward field devices — occur at human-perceptible timescales, where authorization evaluation latency of tens or hundreds of milliseconds is operationally acceptable. Control loops at the field device level may operate at timescales where even single-digit millisecond latencies introduced by intermediate processing are unacceptable. Section 05's analysis of the edge enforcement point identified this boundary as carrying the tightest latency constraints in the deployment; the local policy cache is the architecture's primary mechanism for keeping per-command evaluation within that budget without requiring a round-trip to the central policy engine on each request.

The architecture's enforcement placement strategy — positioning enforcement at trust boundary crossings, not inside time-critical control loops — is designed to accommodate this spectrum. But the boundary between supervisory-layer and field-level operations is not always sharp, and the enforcement points positioned at that boundary must operate within latency budgets that are defined by the operational requirements of the systems on the far side, not by what the authorization infrastructure finds convenient to provide.

Authorization evaluation against a local policy cache is substantially faster than a round-trip query to a remote policy engine, but the evaluation latency of a local policy engine depends on the complexity of the policy being evaluated. A simple role-based policy that matches a subject role against a small set of permitted actions can be evaluated in microseconds. A context-aware policy that requires joining subject attributes, resource attributes, time-of-day conditions, and operational mode flags may require substantially more processing. As policy complexity grows — as organizations add finer-grained rules to address specific operational requirements — the per-evaluation latency of the local enforcement point may grow in ways that exceed the latency budget at that boundary.

Policy complexity is not a freely adjustable parameter. It is driven by the operational requirements that the policy must express, and those requirements tend to grow over time as organizations encounter access control scenarios that their initial policy did not anticipate. Enforcement point implementations must be profiled for evaluation latency under realistic policy complexity, and that profiling must be repeated as policy evolves. An enforcement point that meets its latency budget at initial deployment may not meet it after eighteen months of policy evolution.

Deterministic authorization behavior is a related but distinct requirement. OT control systems are often designed with the expectation that their operational components will respond predictably to the same inputs — that a given command will produce the same outcome consistently. An authorization layer that introduces non-deterministic latency, or that produces different decisions for the same inputs depending on the state of the local cache, violates the determinism assumptions that OT system integrators and operators rely on. Context-aware authorization is particularly susceptible to this concern: a policy that produces different outcomes depending on the current time, the current operational mode, or the current state of an external data source introduces decision variability that operators cannot reason about as reliably as the deterministic field-level behavior they are accustomed to.

## Fail-Open vs Fail-Closed Tradeoffs

Every enforcement point must have a defined behavior for failure conditions it cannot resolve — loss of connectivity to the policy engine when the local policy cache has expired, internal component failure, resource exhaustion, or certificate expiration that prevents the enforcement point from authenticating itself to the policy distribution channel. The choice between fail-open behavior, which permits all requests when the enforcement point cannot evaluate them, and fail-closed behavior, which denies all requests, is not a security question with a universal correct answer. It is a site-specific tradeoff between security risk and operational risk.

Fail-closed behavior is the safer default from a security perspective: if the enforcement point cannot verify authorization, it denies access. But in OT environments, denial of access to a field device during an enforcement point failure can have consequences that range from inconvenient to dangerous. An operator who cannot issue an emergency setpoint change because the enforcement point is in a fail-closed state is not in a better position than an operator who can. If the field system is managing a process where human intervention may be required under failure conditions, fail-closed authorization enforcement may directly conflict with the operational safety model.

Fail-open behavior avoids the operational access problem but creates a window during which any subject with network access — not merely authorized ones — can issue commands to field devices. Whether this window is acceptable depends on the threat model and the physical access controls that prevent unauthorized network access during enforcement point failure. In environments with strong network segmentation and physical security, fail-open behavior during a brief enforcement point failure may be an acceptable operational tradeoff. In environments where the authorization layer is the primary access control mechanism, it may not be.

Neither choice eliminates risk; the choice determines which risk the system accepts under failure. What is not acceptable is leaving the choice unspecified, or assuming it will be configured correctly by whoever deploys the enforcement component without explicit guidance. The fail behavior must be documented, reviewed by operations, security, and safety stakeholders, tested under realistic failure conditions, and understood by the operators who will be working with the system when it fails. Critically, the organizational question of who has authority to approve a fail-open exception for a specific enforcement point — and under what conditions — must be resolved before deployment, not during an incident. In most OT organizations, no existing team owns this decision by default: it falls at the intersection of facility operations, OT engineering, and information security, each of which may apply different risk criteria to the same scenario.

Intermediate behaviors — degraded operation modes that permit a narrowed scope of operations rather than all or nothing — introduce additional implementation complexity but may better reflect the operational reality that some commands are safe under failure conditions and others are not. An enforcement point in a degraded mode might permit read operations against field devices while denying write and command operations, allowing operators to observe system state without issuing potentially unsafe commands. Implementing this correctly requires that the enforcement point can distinguish command types during a state where it cannot perform full policy evaluation — a requirement that pushes semantic classification logic into the failure path rather than the normal evaluation path.

## Emergency Override and Supervisory Control

Every OT authorization architecture must accommodate emergency conditions in which the normal authorization workflow is operationally inappropriate. A fire suppression system that requires an authorization decision before issuing a suppression command, or a building emergency where an operator needs to override setpoints across multiple zones immediately, cannot be governed by the same authorization path as routine maintenance access. The existence of emergency override mechanisms is not a gap in the authorization model — it is an operational requirement that the authorization model must explicitly accommodate.

Emergency override procedures that are designed ad hoc, or that amount to "disable the authorization system when things get urgent," undermine the operational integrity and audit coverage that the authorization architecture provides. Every use of an emergency override procedure should produce an audit record that is at least as complete as a normal authorization event: who initiated the override, under what declared conditions, at what time, and what commands were issued during the override period. The override procedure is not an escape from accountability; it is a different authorization pathway with different evaluation logic.

The governance challenge with emergency overrides is that the conditions that make them necessary are also the conditions under which careful process compliance is hardest to maintain. A defined override procedure that requires a supervisor's approval, a secondary confirmation, and a time-limited credential issuance is appropriate for planned emergencies. For an unplanned emergency unfolding in real time, the procedure may be bypassed simply because there is no time to complete it. The authorization architecture must define a procedure that operators will actually follow under realistic emergency conditions — not one that is theoretically complete but practically unusable.

The ownership question for emergency override design is not straightforward. Facility operations teams typically prioritize minimizing barriers to intervention; OT security teams prioritize ensuring that override events produce complete audit records; CISO organizations may have compliance requirements that constrain how override credentials are issued and revoked. These perspectives do not naturally converge to a single procedure design, and the disagreement tends to surface during incident response rather than during design review. Defining override governance requires convening these stakeholders deliberately before deployment — not because the architecture mandates a specific outcome, but because the authorization architecture makes explicit a governance gap that previously existed without formal structure.

Supervisory control systems that issue autonomous commands — the BMS scheduling setpoint adjustments, load-shedding logic responding to demand conditions, reactive responses to sensor thresholds — must also be addressed within the authorization model. These systems are subjects in the authorization model, carrying system identity rather than operator identity. But when they operate autonomously during a connectivity gap, or when their programming leads them to issue commands that a human operator would not have approved, the authorization model must define how such commands are handled. Allowing autonomous BMS operations to proceed without authorization evaluation during a connectivity gap may be operationally necessary; allowing them to proceed without any audit record is a separate and avoidable gap.

## Credential and Certificate Lifecycle Management

The identity-aware authorization model depends on cryptographic credentials throughout: operator identity tokens, device certificates for gateways and enforcement points, the certificates used by the policy engine to authenticate itself to enforcement points, and the signing keys used by the identity provider to issue tokens. Each of these credential types has a lifecycle — issuance, rotation, expiration, and revocation — and lifecycle management at production scale introduces operational complexity that the conceptual architecture largely defers.

Certificate rotation is a routine operation in enterprise IT environments that has become operationally tractable through automation. In OT environments, it is substantially less tractable. Field gateways and enforcement points deployed at edge sites may not be reachable through the same management channels as enterprise devices. Certificate rotation procedures that require physical access to the device, that require coordinated downtime of the enforcement point, or that require manual steps at the site create maintenance overhead that scales linearly with deployment size. Automation of certificate lifecycle management requires that devices support the relevant management protocols — SCEP, EST, or equivalent — and that those protocols are accessible through the network topology of the deployment. Neither can be assumed for the installed base of OT infrastructure.

Certificate expiration that is not caught and addressed before the expiration date causes enforcement points to lose their ability to authenticate to the policy distribution channel, validate the policy engine's identity, or accept new policy updates. An enforcement point with an expired certificate operating against a stale local policy cache is in a degraded state that may be difficult to detect remotely and operationally problematic to resolve. NIST SP 800-82 Rev. 3 explicitly identifies certificate management as an operational challenge in ICS deployments [NIST SP 800-82, Rev. 3], and the addition of an authorization layer that depends on certificates at multiple points in the architecture does not simplify this challenge.

The device identity model introduces a further dimension of lifecycle complexity. Gateways and enforcement points that are replaced — because of hardware failure, end-of-life hardware refresh, or site reconfiguration — must be re-enrolled in the identity infrastructure before they can participate in the authorization system. The enrollment process for a replacement device must produce the same effective authorization behavior as the device it replaces, including the policy state it had synchronized, without requiring that the replacement device inherit the same long-lived cryptographic keys as its predecessor. Key portability is not a property to be preserved; the replacement device should receive fresh credentials. But the operational transition from old to new device must not leave a window during which the replacement is operating without authorization coverage.

## Fleet-Scale Resource and Identity Management

The BASIS PoC validated the authorization model against five simulated resources. A production BAS deployment may involve hundreds of controllers, thousands of monitored and controllable points, and dozens of operator and technician identities with different role assignments, site-specific access scopes, and varying authentication requirements. The gap between these scales is not merely quantitative — it is qualitative, because the operational tooling and processes required to manage identity and resource configuration at fleet scale do not naturally emerge from the patterns that work at small scale.

Resource descriptor management — defining and maintaining the structured identifiers that map physical controller points to the authorization model's resource vocabulary — requires a process that keeps those identifiers current as device configurations change. A BAS controller that is reconfigured to add points, change register assignments, or modify the semantics of existing objects requires corresponding updates to the adapter normalization and the resource descriptor registry. If those updates are not made, the enforcement point continues evaluating authorization requests against a resource model that no longer accurately reflects the device. The mismatch is not directly observable at the policy level — the policy engine continues making well-formed decisions — but the decisions are being made about the wrong resources.

Maintaining this correspondence at fleet scale requires tooling that the conceptual architecture does not specify: a resource registry that is versioned and auditable, a process for synchronizing resource descriptors with device configuration changes, and a mechanism for surfacing normalization discrepancies before they affect authorization decisions. These are operational infrastructure requirements, not architectural gaps, but they are requirements that must be filled before the authorization model can be operated reliably at scale.

Identity management at scale introduces similar challenges. Adding a new operator, modifying a technician's site scope, onboarding a vendor service account, or removing access for a departed employee are routine identity lifecycle events that must be handled through a defined process with appropriate review and audit coverage. In small deployments, these operations may be performed manually. At fleet scale, manual identity lifecycle management becomes the operational bottleneck that drives the adoption of shared credentials and provisioning shortcuts — exactly the patterns that identity-aware authorization is designed to replace.

Automated identity lifecycle management — provisioning through HR system integrations, role assignment through group membership in enterprise directories, deprovisioning through automated offboarding workflows — reduces manual overhead and improves consistency, but introduces its own operational dependencies. The authorization system becomes dependent on the accuracy of the enterprise directory as a source of truth for operator attributes. An operator whose directory account reflects stale role assignments receives authorization decisions based on those stale assignments. The identity provider does not independently verify that the attributes it is issuing tokens for are operationally correct; it trusts the directory.

## Vendor Interoperability and Protocol Diversity

OT environments are characteristically heterogeneous. A single building may contain BACnet/IP controllers from one manufacturer, Modbus RTU devices from another, LonWorks-based equipment from a third, and proprietary-protocol devices from a fourth. Each of these protocol families has its own object model, its own communication semantics, and its own capability constraints. The protocol adapter layer is designed to absorb this heterogeneity by normalizing all protocol-specific communication into the shared subject-resource-action representation. In practice, the scope of what adapters must handle is substantially larger than the two-protocol PoC implementation suggests. As Section 06 noted, the BASIS PoC exercised its adapter abstraction against simulated MQTT and Modbus TCP resources under controlled conditions with a fixed, well-defined resource set — conditions that are not representative of the protocol diversity, vendor-specific behavioral variations, and object model complexity present in real BAS deployments.

Protocol standards define a shared base, but OT device manufacturers commonly implement proprietary extensions, non-standard object configurations, and vendor-specific behavior that complies with the letter of the standard while diverging in ways that affect adapter normalization. A BACnet WriteProperty request on one manufacturer's controller may map cleanly to the authorization model's action vocabulary; on another manufacturer's device of nominally the same type, the same operation may use a different object structure, different property semantics, or a vendor-specific extension property that the adapter does not recognize. Comprehensive adapter coverage requires per-device or per-manufacturer characterization work that is not derivable from protocol specifications alone.

Firmware updates introduce adapter compatibility concerns that have no parallel in IT software systems. When a controller manufacturer releases a firmware update that changes the object model, adds new property types, or modifies the behavioral semantics of existing commands, the adapter that was correct before the update may no longer be correct after it. In IT systems, software component updates are typically managed with version compatibility requirements that can be tested before deployment. In OT environments, firmware updates may be applied by building maintenance personnel who are not aware of the authorization infrastructure dependency, and the compatibility impact may not be discovered until the adapter produces incorrect normalizations for a changed device.

This creates a change management dependency between the OT device layer and the authorization infrastructure that neither the device operators nor the authorization system administrators may own. Establishing a process that routes firmware updates through an adapter compatibility review is operationally straightforward to describe and organizationally difficult to maintain, particularly in multi-owner environments where the teams responsible for BAS maintenance and the teams responsible for authorization infrastructure operate under different organizational structures and change management disciplines. In practice, the BAS vendor or integrator who applies a firmware update may have no awareness that an authorization adapter depends on the pre-update device behavior; the team operating the authorization infrastructure may have no visibility into the firmware change until downstream normalization failures surface. No natural owner exists for this cross-domain dependency in most organizational structures, which means its detection defaults to operational monitoring after the fact rather than proactive review before deployment.

## Audit Durability and Delivery Semantics

The audit pipeline must provide durability guarantees that the conceptual architecture specifies but leaves largely unquantified: audit events must not be lost, they must be delivered to the central audit store in a form that supports forensic analysis, and the record must be complete enough that an absence of events during a time window can be distinguished from an absence of activity during that window. Section 06 acknowledged that the BASIS PoC's audit store was administratively collocated with the operational components it covered and did not implement out-of-band delivery, administrative separation, or cryptographic chaining — properties explicitly required for production audit integrity that the PoC deferred in favor of development tractability. Production deployment must address both the delivery semantics and the administrative independence that the PoC's SQLite-backed implementation did not exercise.

Durability at the edge is a function of local buffer capacity and delivery reliability. When an edge enforcement point is unable to deliver audit events to the central store — because of a network outage, a central store unavailability event, or a transport layer fault — those events must buffer locally. The local buffer is finite. If the connectivity gap extends beyond the buffer's capacity, events must be either dropped or handled according to a defined overflow policy. Dropping events silently is unacceptable — it creates an audit gap that is indistinguishable from a period of no activity. An overflow policy that generates an explicit alert on buffer exhaustion makes the gap observable, but it requires that the monitoring infrastructure that would receive that alert is itself reachable.

Delivery ordering is a distinct concern from delivery durability. Events that buffer locally and are delivered in batch when connectivity is restored arrive at the central store out of temporal order relative to events from other enforcement points that were delivered in real time. Audit analysis tools that assume temporally ordered event streams may produce incorrect results when applied to a record that contains reordered events from partially disconnected sites. The audit store must either enforce ordering at ingestion or provide analysis tools that are robust to reordered delivery. Neither is free — reordering at ingestion requires buffering and delay, and analysis tools that handle reordered events are more complex to implement correctly.

Audit integrity across administrative zones is a property that the architecture identifies as important but that is operationally expensive to implement. Cryptographic chaining of audit records — where each record contains a hash of its predecessor — provides tamper evidence but requires that the enforcement point maintain the chain state locally and that the central store can verify chain integrity across records from multiple enforcement points. The chain must be maintained consistently during normal operation, local buffering, and bulk delivery after a connectivity gap. A chain that is broken during bulk delivery provides weaker tamper evidence than a continuously maintained one.

ISA/IEC 62443-2-1 identifies audit and accountability as a foundational security requirement for IACS deployments, including the requirement that audit records be protected against modification and unauthorized access [ISA/IEC 62443-2-1, 2010]. Meeting this requirement in a distributed OT deployment with intermittent connectivity requires audit infrastructure design decisions that go substantially beyond what an enterprise-focused audit system provides by default.

## Operational Maintenance and Change Management

Policy changes in a distributed enforcement architecture are not instantaneous. A policy change authored at the Central Services Zone must be distributed to every enforcement point in the deployment before it takes effect universally. During the distribution window, some enforcement points operate on the new policy and others operate on the old — a transient state of divergence across the enforcement fleet that is structurally identical to the cache inconsistency discussed in the policy synchronization analysis above. The operational implications of this transition period depend on the nature of the change.

A policy change that narrows access — revoking a vendor's ability to issue a command, restricting an operator's scope to a subset of sites — may appear to take effect partially during the distribution window. An operator or vendor whose access has been narrowed may still succeed at some enforcement points and fail at others, depending on which have received the updated policy. From the operator's perspective, this produces inconsistent behavior that may be difficult to diagnose without knowledge of the policy distribution infrastructure. Designing policy changes to be safe during the partial-distribution window — rather than assuming that distribution completes instantaneously — requires both policy authoring discipline and tooling that makes the distribution status of each enforcement point visible.

Change management for the authorization infrastructure itself — policy engine updates, enforcement point software updates, adapter updates — must be integrated with the OT change management process rather than managed independently. OT environments typically follow rigorous change management procedures that require review, approval, and scheduled maintenance windows for any change to operational systems [NIST SP 800-82, Rev. 3]. Authorization infrastructure updates that are deployed outside the change management process — because they are treated as IT infrastructure rather than OT infrastructure — may bypass the review and testing that would catch a misconfiguration before it affects operational systems.

Maintenance windows in OT environments are planned, infrequent, and operationally constrained. The window available for authorization infrastructure maintenance may be measured in hours per month, and the operations that must be completed within that window — certificate rotations, adapter updates, policy engine version upgrades — may exceed what the window allows. Prioritizing among maintenance operations, deferring some updates until future windows, and managing the operational risk of running partially updated infrastructure between windows are routine operational disciplines in OT environments, and they must be applied to authorization infrastructure as deliberately as they are applied to the field systems it governs.

## Monitoring, Observability, and Failure Detection

An authorization layer that is not observable is an authorization layer that cannot be operated reliably. Detecting a failure, diagnosing its cause, and determining whether it represents a security event or an operational one requires that the authorization infrastructure produce monitoring signals that are distinct from the operational OT telemetry it sits alongside.

The monitoring requirements for the authorization layer span several categories. Component health monitoring — whether the policy engine, identity provider, and enforcement points are running and responding correctly — is the baseline. Policy distribution monitoring — whether each enforcement point has received current policy within its defined synchronization window — is necessary for detecting cache staleness before it becomes operationally significant. Audit delivery monitoring — whether events are flowing from enforcement points to the central store, and whether local buffers at edge sites are filling — is required for maintaining confidence in audit record completeness. Authorization decision monitoring — patterns in allow and deny decisions across the deployment — provides visibility into whether policy is behaving as expected under real traffic.

Each of these monitoring concerns requires instrumentation that enforcement components must implement and that the monitoring infrastructure must interpret. Monitoring instrumentation in enforcement components must be designed to remain accurate during the failure conditions it is intended to detect — an enforcement component that is failing in a way that also corrupts its health reporting will appear healthy to monitoring systems while behaving incorrectly under real traffic.

The operational integration of authorization monitoring with existing OT monitoring infrastructure introduces organizational complexity. OT environments typically have established monitoring systems — building management dashboards, SCADA historian systems, alarm management infrastructure — that are operated by facility management teams. Authorization infrastructure monitoring is closer in character to IT network operations monitoring than to BAS operational monitoring. Integrating the two requires either extending existing OT monitoring systems to cover IT-style infrastructure health, or maintaining separate monitoring systems for the authorization layer, with the corresponding risk that faults in one system are not visible to the operators responsible for the other.

## Administrative Ownership and Governance Complexity

The authorization layer spans organizational and administrative boundaries in ways that no existing OT infrastructure component does. The identity provider is administered by whoever manages enterprise IAM. The policy engine is administered by whoever governs access policy for the OT environment. The enforcement points are deployed in OT zones that may be administered by facility management teams, building automation subcontractors, or the OT operations group. The protocol adapters contain configuration that reflects the device object models maintained by the BAS integrators who installed and configured the field devices.

No single team currently owns all of these concerns in most OT organizations, and the authorization architecture creates dependencies between them that did not previously exist. A change to the BAS controller configuration may require a corresponding adapter update that requires review by the authorization infrastructure team. A policy change to restrict a vendor's access requires that the vendor's identity in the identity provider accurately reflects their current engagement scope — a data quality requirement that spans IAM, procurement, and OT operations. A certificate rotation for an edge enforcement point requires coordinated maintenance windows from both the OT site operations team and the authorization infrastructure team.

These cross-team dependencies are not unique to OT authorization; they arise wherever security infrastructure is layered above operational systems that were originally managed independently. But the consequence of unresolved coordination failures is more significant in OT environments, where operational continuity requirements are less forgiving and where the teams involved may have limited experience operating infrastructure that spans IT and OT administrative domains. The Purdue Reference Model's organizational layering — a long-established framework for thinking about OT administrative domains — does not address the cross-layer coordination requirements that authorization infrastructure introduces [Purdue, 1992].

Ownership fragmentation becomes acutely visible during failure scenarios. When an enforcement point enters a degraded state, the decision about which behavior is acceptable — fail-open, fail-closed, or a limited read-only mode — requires input from the OT site operations team who owns the operational continuity requirement, the OT security team who owns the authorization policy, and in some cases the facility safety manager who owns the life-safety implications of access denial. None of these teams individually holds all the context needed to make the decision correctly, and in many organizations there is no standing coordination path that brings them together outside scheduled governance forums. The result is that degraded mode behavior often defaults to the enforcement component's compile-time default rather than a deliberately chosen operational posture.

Policy rollback presents a comparable ownership gap. If a policy change produces incorrect authorization behavior — denying legitimate operations, or permitting operations that should be restricted — reversing it requires coordinated action from the team that authored the policy, the team that operated the distribution pipeline, and the teams operating the enforcement points that received it. In a deployment with enforcement points across multiple sites, determining which points have received the new policy, reversing the distribution to each, and verifying that all affected points have returned to the previous state is an operational recovery procedure that must be defined and rehearsed before it is needed. Organizations that have not established and tested a policy rollback procedure may find it difficult to execute under the time pressure of an active incident, particularly where the teams involved operate under different change management authorities.

Synchronization failures — where an enforcement point stops receiving policy updates within its expected window — require a response that spans the team operating the policy engine, the team responsible for network connectivity between the policy engine and the edge site, and the team responsible for the enforcement point hardware. Each team may apply different incident severity criteria to the same symptom. Establishing response ownership and escalation paths for synchronization failures as part of the deployment design, rather than as an improvised response to the first occurrence, is an operational requirement that the authorization architecture surfaces but cannot fulfill.

## Degraded Operation Modes

Failure conditions in a distributed authorization system produce a spectrum of degraded states rather than a single binary between fully operational and completely failed. An enforcement point whose local policy cache has expired is in a different operational state than one that is processing requests correctly against current policy. A deployment where three of fifteen enforcement points have lost connectivity to the policy engine is in a different state than a deployment where all fifteen are synchronized. Operations teams must be able to reason about what the authorization system is doing during degraded states, not only during normal operation.

Defining degraded operation modes explicitly — specifying which capabilities remain available, which are suspended, and what signals indicate which mode is in effect — is a prerequisite for operating the authorization layer responsibly in OT environments. A deployment where degraded modes are undefined is a deployment where operations teams will improvise their response to partial failures, producing inconsistent and potentially unsafe behaviors across sites and incidents. As the administrative ownership analysis below discusses, the team responsible for defining acceptable degraded behaviors is rarely obvious within existing OT organizational structures, which means that the definition work must be explicitly assigned during deployment planning rather than assumed to emerge from normal operations governance.

Degraded mode design must also address the interaction between authorization degradation and the operational safety systems of the OT environment. An authorization layer that enters a fail-closed degraded mode during a network partition does not interact with the field device safety instrumentation; the safety systems continue operating independently. But if operators interpret the fail-closed authorization state as evidence of a broader system problem and begin bypassing authorization controls through emergency procedures, those bypass actions may produce audit gaps precisely during the period when the system's behavior is most operationally significant.

## Architectural Tradeoffs and Unresolved Tensions

The analysis above surfaces several tensions that the architecture cannot resolve and that each deployment must negotiate explicitly.

The tension between security and continuity is most visible in the fail behavior design and the staleness limit setting. Maximizing security would suggest fail-closed behavior and short staleness limits; maximizing continuity would suggest fail-open behavior and long limits. Neither extreme is operationally appropriate, and the appropriate middle ground depends on the specific threat model and operational requirements of the deployment. Different sites within the same organization may legitimately require different settings.

The tension between policy currency and operational independence is most visible in cache synchronization — a distributed systems consistency tradeoff that the architecture makes explicit by design rather than concealing. An enforcement point that requires frequent synchronization with the central policy engine is more responsive to policy changes but less operationally independent. An enforcement point that can operate for extended periods on cached policy is more independent but may enforce significantly stale decisions. For deployments at geographically remote sites with limited connectivity, extended cached operation may be the only realistic mode; for deployments with reliable connectivity, more frequent synchronization is achievable. Many production deployments will intentionally accept bounded policy inconsistency across the enforcement fleet in exchange for operational continuity — not as a compromise position but as a deliberate choice to prioritize availability over strict consistency in an environment where the cost of unavailability is measured in operational impact. The synchronization window is then a tunable operational parameter rather than a defect to eliminate.

The tension between audit completeness and delivery reliability is most visible at edge sites with intermittent connectivity. Audit events that cannot be delivered in real time are locally buffered, but buffering is finite, reordering is unavoidable, and delivery can fail entirely if conditions are severe enough. Accepting these audit limitations in exchange for operational continuity is a deliberate tradeoff. It must be documented as such, not treated as a temporary imperfection that will be resolved by better infrastructure.

The tension between policy expressiveness and evaluation determinism is most visible as policy complexity grows. Policies that express nuanced operational distinctions through context-aware conditions are more precise but less predictable in their evaluation behavior. Simpler policies are more predictable but may require compensating controls to address access scenarios they cannot express precisely. Neither pole is universally correct, and the right balance depends on the operational vocabulary of the specific deployment and the sophistication of the teams who will author and maintain the policy.

These tensions are not engineering defects that a sufficiently sophisticated implementation resolves. They are fundamental properties of distributed systems operating under the CAP theorem's constraints — where partition tolerance is a given in OT networks with intermittent connectivity, and where the deployment must choose how to distribute the residual tension between consistency and availability. The architecture described in this paper provides a framework within which these tradeoffs can be made deliberately, documented clearly, and owned by the teams who must live with them. It does not eliminate the tradeoffs, and no architectural refinement will.

---

## Summary

The conceptual architecture for identity-aware authorization in OT environments is coherent. Its components interact as described, its authorization flows are implementable, and the BASIS PoC demonstrated that the core mechanisms hold at limited scope. What the conceptual architecture defers — and what this section has examined — is the operational engineering required to deploy, operate, and sustain that architecture in production.

Production deployment introduces high availability requirements for components whose failure modes were abstracted away in the conceptual description. It introduces policy distribution problems that have no satisfying solution when connectivity is genuinely unreliable. It introduces credential lifecycle management overhead that scales with deployment size and that has no natural owner in most OT organizations. It introduces change management coordination requirements that span administrative domains not accustomed to coordinating. It introduces failure modes whose correct handling requires site-specific judgments about the relative weight of security risk and operational risk.

None of these challenges are unique to identity-aware authorization. They are the normal operational challenges of any distributed security infrastructure deployed in constrained environments. What makes them particularly consequential in OT contexts is the operational sensitivity of the systems they govern, the long lifecycle of the infrastructure they must coexist with, and the limited tolerance for operational disruption that characterizes the environments they are intended to improve.

An organization that evaluates identity-aware authorization purely on the basis of its conceptual architecture will underestimate the deployment investment required and will be unprepared for the operational problems that production deployment surfaces. An organization that evaluates it on the basis of the full operational engineering picture — including the problems identified in this section — is in a position to make a realistic judgment about where deployment effort is warranted and what tradeoffs are acceptable for their specific environment.

The architecture is a starting point for that judgment, not a conclusion.
